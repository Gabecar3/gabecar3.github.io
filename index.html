About Me

I’m Gabe Carvalho, an Aerospace Engineer with a Bachelor’s degree from Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University. My background spans manufacturing, design, testing, V&V, and spacecraft processing, with a strong focus on safety through innovation. I’ve led modification and V&V projects and supported Underway Recovery Training aboard the USS Somerset for Artemis II. As a Lean Six Sigma Green Belt, I bring sharp problem-solving skills, fast learning, and effective leadership to every challenge. I’m passionate about pushing the boundaries of aerospace, and outside of work, I enjoy video games, working on cars, and building things as a maker.

Projects

Artemis II - Orion C-17 Transportation

To enable post-flight transport of the Orion spacecraft from Naval Air Station North Island to Kennedy Space Center, the Orion Recovery Cradle required modification to meet U.S. Air Force requirements for transporting equipment with hazardous commodities aboard a C-17 aircraft. I supported the development of the requirements matrix and led the modification as well as verification and validation (V&V) operations to ensure full compliance and successful project completion.

Artemis II - Underway Recovery Training 12 (URT-12)

Participated in Underway Recovery Training aboard the USS Somerset, living aboard the ship for a week while supporting Artemis II mission readiness. Led readiness verification of critical ground support equipment (GSE) prior to deployment, resolving non-conformances to ensure operational success. Conducted pre-task briefings to communicate mission objectives and safety protocols, promoting situational awareness and team preparedness. Following the mission, led deconfiguration of equipment installed on the Somerset, preparing it for storage at Naval Base San Diego or transport back to Kennedy Space Center.

Vee-Tail Static Stability Analysis

Developed longitudinal, directional, and lateral static stability equations for Vee-tail empennage aircraft. Validated the equations with vortex lattice CFD, demonstrating agreement within ±5%, supporting their accuracy and reliability.

Unmanned Fire Fighting Aircraft - Aircraft Detail Design

As project manager for a team of six engineers, I led the design of an unmanned fire-fighting aircraft inspired by the Boeing MG-25 Ariel Refueling Drone, aimed to compete with the CL-415. The aircraft was designed to deliver 2,000 gallons of fire suppressant with a range of 500 nautical miles. I oversaw the detailed design of the wings and conducted Damage Tolerance Analysis to establish inspection intervals and determine component lifespan. I also completed the airworthiness and loading analysis of the aircraft to determine structural design requirements. Our design, The Ember-Eagle was selected as the best aircraft of the semester, highlighting the success of the project.

Military Training Aircraft - Aircraft Preliminary Design

Led the preliminary design of a twin-jet engine military trainer inspired by the A-10 Warthog, with specific requirements for endurance, rate of climb, max speed, landing distance, and the twin DGEN 380 engines. Despite challenges due to incorrect engine weight data, the team worked together to correct the errors just one week before final presentations. The aircraft, affectionately named "Cinderblock," placed second in the course and received a commendation from the dean of the Aerospace Engineering department for our exceptional redesign efforts in response to the initial inaccurate requirements.
